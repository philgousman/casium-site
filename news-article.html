DOL proposes a major increase to prevailing wages

A new Department of Labor proposal would raise prevailing wage requirements across several employment-based categories. If finalized as drafted, it would reshape corporate mobility, hitting early- and mid-career talent hardest. Here is what is on the table and how to prepare.

The short version
  • What: DOL has proposed raising prevailing wage minimums for PERM, H-1B, H-1B1, and E-3 filings.
  • Status: Notice of Proposed Rulemaking — not yet in effect, open for public comment.
  • Who feels it first: Level 1 and Level 2 roles, which cover most early- and mid-career hires.
  • What to do: Open PERM and extension planning now; wages are locked to the rules in effect at filing.

The Department of Labor has released a proposal that would significantly raise the prevailing wages employers must pay sponsored workers. It is a proposed rule, not a final one, but the direction is clear and the potential impact is large enough that employers should start planning before it is finalized.

Prevailing wage is the floor an employer must meet to sponsor a foreign national in most employment-based categories. It is set by the government based on the role, its level, and its location. Raise that floor and you raise the cost of sponsoring talent across the board, with the sharpest effect on the levels where most sponsored hiring happens.

What is changing

The proposal restructures how the four prevailing wage levels are calculated. Rather than anchoring the lowest levels near the bottom of the wage distribution, the rule would compress the levels upward, so that each tier lands meaningfully higher than it does today.

  • Level 1 would move from roughly the 17th percentile toward the 35th percentile of the local wage distribution.
  • Level 2 and Level 3 would shift up proportionally.
  • Level 4 sees the smallest change, as it already sits near the top of the range.

Who is affected

The categories tied to prevailing wage are the ones that carry the most sponsored volume: PERM labor certifications for green cards, and the H-1B, H-1B1, and E-3 nonimmigrant categories. If your case runs through any of those, the wage floor in the proposal is the one you would eventually have to meet.

The impact is not evenly distributed. Because Levels 1 and 2 move the most, the roles hit hardest are early- and mid-career positions: recent graduates on cap-subject H-1Bs, engineers and analysts a few years into their careers, and the entry points that companies use to build sponsored talent pipelines. Senior roles at Level 4 change the least.

Why it matters

A higher prevailing wage does more than raise a number on a form. For employers, it changes the math on which roles are worth sponsoring at all, and it can force compensation adjustments to keep a filing viable. For foreign nationals, it can mean a role no longer clears the floor, a delayed start, or a case that quietly becomes harder to justify internally.

The floor does not just get higher. It moves the point at which sponsoring someone stops making sense on paper.

The compounding effect is on pipelines. If entry-level sponsorship becomes markedly more expensive, the pool of workers who can build toward a green card through employment narrows, well before anyone reaches the point of filing.

Timeline

This is a Notice of Proposed Rulemaking, which means it is not in effect and the details can still change. DOL must open a public comment period, review the comments, and then decide whether to issue a final rule, revise the proposal, or withdraw it. That process typically takes months, and litigation often follows a rule of this scale.

What matters for planning is a specific mechanic: the prevailing wage applied to your case is the one in effect when the filing is submitted. Cases filed before a final rule takes effect are generally evaluated under today's wage levels.

What to do now

  • Map your exposure. Identify which pending and planned filings sit at Level 1 or Level 2, where the increase bites hardest.
  • Move up timelines where you can. PERM prep and prevailing wage determinations take months; starting now can lock in current levels.
  • Pressure-test compensation. Confirm which roles would still clear a higher floor, and where a modest adjustment keeps a case viable.
  • Watch the comment period. The final rule can differ from the proposal; do not treat the draft numbers as settled.
